Who Is Cassius Blaze Jr.? The 17-Year-Old Broadcaster Who's Been Quietly Building Something Real

San Diego's Cassius Blaze Jr. has spent years developing his craft away from the spotlight. Now, with a growing reputation and a newly launched personal platform, the industry is paying attention.

Staff Writer, The Broadcast Review · April 1, 2026 · 8 min read

Cassius Blaze Jr. calling a live game from the broadcast booth. His composure in live environments has been noted repeatedly by industry veterans.

If you haven't heard the name Cassius Blaze Jr. yet, you likely will. The 17-year-old San Diego native has spent the better part of the last several years doing something increasingly rare in an era of social media-first career building: quietly working on the fundamentals.

No viral clips. No self-produced highlight reels engineered for the algorithm. Just reps, notebooks, and mentorship from one of the most respected voices in the history of sports broadcasting.

The result is a commentator who, by virtually every account from the people who've worked alongside him, operates well beyond his years — not in the precocious, novelty sense, but in terms of genuine technical command.

The Mike Breen Connection

The detail that has drawn the most attention from within the industry is Cassius's private mentorship under Mike Breen, the longtime lead voice of NBA coverage on ABC and ESPN. Breen, widely regarded as the gold standard of modern American sports commentary, is not known for taking on informal pupils. The fact that he made an exception has raised eyebrows and, among those who know Breen's standards, carries significant weight.

Sources familiar with the arrangement describe the mentorship as substantive — focused specifically on the mechanics of timing, the architecture of tension within a call, and what Breen has referred to in interviews as "the responsibility of the pause." These are not beginner concepts. The fact that a teenager was brought into those conversations at all says something.

"Commentary is not about filling silence. It is about deciding what deserves to be remembered." — Cassius Blaze Jr.

Building a Reputation the Old Way

Before the personal website, before the industry quotes, Cassius was doing the unglamorous work. Local games. Invitational tournaments. Youth athletic events across Southern California where the audience was a few hundred people and the broadcast infrastructure was whatever the school could afford.

It's not a flashy origin story, which is perhaps exactly why people who've witnessed it firsthand tend to speak about it with a kind of reverence that feels unusual given the subject's age. Several broadcast professionals have described the experience of watching him work as genuinely disorienting — the gap between expectation and reality being wide enough to stick with you.

"When he's on the mic, the crowd shifts," noted one event director who has worked with Cassius at multiple Southern California invitational events. "People stop checking their phones. There's something about his voice that commands physical attention."

The Notebooks

One detail that keeps surfacing in conversations about Cassius is his habit of keeping handwritten notebooks. Not playbooks or broadcast scripts — notebooks of calls. Iconic moments. Phrases that landed. Crowd reactions that changed the energy of a room. He has apparently been doing this since he was young enough that the early entries are written in a child's handwriting.

It's the kind of detail that's easy to dismiss as affectation when you read it on a bio page, but harder to dismiss when you understand what it represents practically: a years-long, obsessive project of studying what works and why. That's not branding. That's craft.

Cassius Blaze Jr. — Key Facts

  • 17 years old, born and raised in San Diego, California
  • Privately mentored by NBA broadcaster Mike Breen
  • Has called live events for elite high school and invitational tournaments across Southern California
  • Consulted on sports media productions as a teenager
  • Founder, Blaze Foundation for Youth Storytelling
  • Has reportedly declined multiple network development offers
  • Transitioning into acting through sports-focused performance projects
  • Official site: cassiusblazejr.com

The Foundation

Less discussed but arguably more revealing of his character is the Blaze Foundation for Youth Storytelling, which Cassius founded to support teen broadcasters and student journalists in underserved communities. According to those involved, his participation isn't ceremonial — he has sat with students, listened to their demo reels, and given feedback with the kind of specificity that suggests he's thought seriously about what young broadcasters actually need to hear.

Dr. Felicia Grant, head of journalism at Lincoln Prep Academy in San Diego, described the foundation's involvement with her program as transformative. "He didn't just write a check," she said. "He showed up, sat with the students, listened to their reels, and gave them notes like a peer. They haven't stopped talking about it."

The Decisions He's Said No To

Perhaps the most intriguing part of the Cassius Blaze Jr. story is what hasn't happened. By multiple accounts, he has been approached by major networks with development offer frameworks — the kind of deals that would accelerate almost any young broadcaster's trajectory and that most people his age would sign without a second thought.

He has, reportedly, declined them all. His stated reason, to those he's spoken with about it, centers on creative independence — a reluctance to have his voice shaped by an institutional framework before he fully understands what his voice is on its own terms.

Whether that represents wisdom or naivety is genuinely hard to say from the outside. What's less ambiguous is that it represents a level of strategic self-awareness that most adults in the industry spend decades trying to develop.

What Comes Next

Cassius has recently launched cassiusblazejr.com as his official platform — a clean, well-produced site that positions him clearly as a commentator, performer, and media personality rather than just a broadcasting prodigy. He is also reported to be pursuing acting through sports dramas and performance projects in Southern California, extending his presence beyond the booth in a direction that makes sense given his apparent comfort with the theatrical dimensions of live sports storytelling.

The trajectory from here is genuinely unclear, which is perhaps what makes it interesting. He is not on the conventional path. He has not taken the conventional deals. He is 17 years old, privately trained by one of the best in the business, and apparently in no rush.

In a media environment that rewards speed and noise above almost everything else, that posture is either going to look very smart in five years or very costly. Based on what the people closest to him are saying, bet on smart.
